The **Zettelkasten** ("slip box") is a method, and an attitude, from the German
sociologist Niklas Luhmann. Over his career he filled a wooden cabinet with around
90,000 index cards, each carrying a single idea and a hand-written address, each
pointing to others. He wrote ~70 books and 400 papers and gave the slip-box the
credit, half-joking that he never forced himself to write — he just asked the box
what it wanted to say next.

The mechanics are simple enough to feel anticlimactic:

- Every card is an [[Atomic notes|atomic note]] — one thought.
- Every card has an ID, so others can reference it precisely.
- New cards link to existing cards. The *links*, not the folders, are the
  organisation.
- An index points at a few entry cards; from there you follow your nose.

What's striking is how exactly this maps onto a website. A card is a page, an ID is
a URL, and a reference is a hyperlink. Add automatic [[Backlinks]] — something
Luhmann had to maintain by hand — and a paper slip-box becomes a digital garden
with almost nothing lost in translation. The cabinet was a hypertext system fifty
years early.
