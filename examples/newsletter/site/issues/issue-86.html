Soft Edges

Sometime in the last decade, the whole interface world rounded its corners. It’s worth asking what we were trying to say.

Pull up a screenshot from 2011 and the thing that dates it isn’t the colour or the gradient — it’s the corners. They’re sharp. Hard ninety-degree turns, like the page was cut with a guillotine. Now look at anything shipped this year. Everything is rounded. Buttons, cards, avatars, the search box, the search box inside the card. We have, collectively and almost without discussion, decided that software should feel like a river stone.

I don’t think that’s an accident, and I don’t think it’s only fashion.

What a corner says

A sharp corner is architectural. It says: this is a document, a form, a serious surface, handle it carefully. A rounded one is bodily. It says: this is a thing, pick it up, it won’t hurt you. As interfaces moved from desks to palms — from things we operated to things we held — the corners softened to match. Rounding is the design language of approachable.

“The radius is small, but the message isn’t: nothing here has a sharp edge to catch you.”

The trap is that softness has a saturation point. Round everything and nothing reads as solid; the whole screen turns to marshmallow and your eye has nowhere firm to land. The skill isn’t rounding. It’s knowing the one or two places to leave a hard edge so the soft ones mean something.
