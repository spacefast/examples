Memory fades on a curve. Hermann Ebbinghaus measured it in the 1880s by memorising
nonsense syllables on himself: forgetting is steep at first, then levels off, and
each time you successfully recall something the curve gets flatter — the memory
decays more slowly than before.

**Spaced repetition** turns that curve into a schedule. Instead of cramming (which
fights the curve and loses), you review a fact at *expanding* intervals — a day,
then three, then a week, then a month — each review timed for the moment just
before you'd have forgotten. Two effects compound:

- **The spacing effect.** The same total study time, spread out, produces far more
  durable memory than the same time massed together.
- **The testing effect.** *Retrieving* a fact strengthens it much more than
  re-reading does. The struggle to recall is the part that works.

This is why [[Evergreen notes]] are titled as claims rather than topics. A note
called "Spacing beats cramming for durable memory" is a tiny self-test every time
you meet it — you can try to reconstruct the argument before you read on. A note
called "memory stuff" asks nothing of you and teaches nothing back. Phrasing a note
well is, sneakily, scheduling a review you'll do every time you stumble across it.
