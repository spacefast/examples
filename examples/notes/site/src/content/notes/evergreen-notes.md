Most notes are disposable: a meeting scrawl, a highlight you'll never reopen, a
to-do you finish and delete. **Evergreen notes** are the opposite. They're written
to *accumulate* — phrased so that the you of next year, working on a different
project, can pick one up and build on it.

Andy Matuschak laid out the properties that make a note evergreen, and they've held
up better than almost anything else I've tried:

1. **Atomic.** One note, one idea — small enough to link precisely. See
   [[Atomic notes]].
2. **Concept-oriented.** Titled by the *idea*, not the source. "Spaced repetition
   exploits the forgetting curve" beats "Notes from chapter 4."
3. **Densely linked.** A note that connects to nothing is a note you'll never find
   again. Linking is filing.
4. **Written for yourself, in your own words.** Rephrasing is where the
   understanding actually happens.

The phrasing matters because of how memory works: a note titled like a *claim* is a
prompt your future self can argue with, and arguing is retrieval, and retrieval is
what makes things stick (that's the bridge to [[Spaced repetition]]).

The discipline is real, though. Evergreen notes are slow to write and easy to skip.
The trick that keeps me honest is [[Tending notes]] — touching old notes a little
every week instead of trying to perfect them on the first pass.
