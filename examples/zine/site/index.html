Dear
reader,

There is a second city that switches on when the first one clocks out. We started OFFCUTS because nobody was making a magazine for it — for the bakers proofing dough at 3 a.m., the sign-fixers up ladders in the rain, the insomniacs who know exactly which corner store still has the lights on.

This first issue, Night Bloom, is printed in three risograph inks — fluorescent pink, federal blue, sunshine yellow — and then again, here, in light. The misregistration is on purpose. So is the grain. We like our pages a little out of true.

Read it top to bottom. It's a stack of spreads, not a feed.

With ink under our nails,
The OFFCUTS desk

Verse · by R. Ortega

SODIUM

The streetlamps hum
a single orange note —
the city's pilot light,
left on so the dark won't catch.

Moths arrive like
tiny, devoted press,
filing the same story
against the glass all night.

And me — awake,
another small machine
burning low so the
morning has something to find lit.

— Rosa Ortega, written on the back of a receipt

Interview № 1

The Neon
Doctor

People call you when their sign dies. What actually goes wrong?

Gas leaks, mostly. A neon tube is just argon and a breath of mercury holding its temper for decades. One hairline crack and the whole letter goes dark. I find the crack by listening — a healthy tube sings, a sick one stutters.

"A sign isn't lighting. It's a promise that someone is still open."

Why do you only work at night?

Because that's when you can see what's wrong. You can't diagnose a glow in daylight. And honestly the city's better company after midnight — quieter, more honest. The signs and I keep the same hours.

Everything's going LED. Does that scare you?

LED is a printout. Neon is handwriting — it bends because a person bent it, it hums because a real gas is excited. People will always want one thing in their life that was made by hand and breathes a little. I'm not worried. I'm booked through spring.

The 24-Hour Laundromat

Found wherever a neighborhood refuses to sleep. Warm, humming, faintly tropical. The unofficial common room of the night shift.

Habitat: corner lots
Specimen 02

The Night Baker

Rises when others fall. Identifiable by flour to the elbow and a thousand-yard stare that smells of cardamom. Active 1 AM–6 AM.

Diet: coffee, dough
Specimen 03

The Moth Convention

Gathers nightly beneath any sodium lamp. No agenda, no minutes, perfect attendance. The only press corps that never sleeps.

Habitat: streetlights
Specimen 04

The Last Bus

A rolling sanctuary, fluorescent and forgiving. Carries poets, closers, and the recently heartbroken to wherever home still is.

Range: city-wide
