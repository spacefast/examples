Paper Brains

I’ve migrated my notes between six apps in ten years. The notes I still use are the ones I wrote on cards.

Here is an embarrassing inventory. In the last decade I have moved my notes into — and then out of — six different apps. Each migration cost a weekend. Each promised to be the last. None were. And every time, a few notes didn’t survive the move: links rotted, formatting broke, an attachment got orphaned. The notes I trust most aren’t in any of those apps. They’re on index cards in a shoebox, and they have never once failed to open.

This isn’t a luddite pitch. I write for a living on a glowing rectangle and I’m not stopping. It’s a pitch about formats that outlive their readers — and a quiet argument that the most futureproof tool you own might be a 3×5 card and a pencil.

The card forces a thought to be one thing

A card has edges. You can’t infinitely scroll it, nest it, or bury it in a folder. One idea per card, in your own words, or it doesn’t fit. That constraint does the thinking. It’s why a corkboard of forty cards beats a document of forty thousand words: you can see the shape of what you know, and rearrange it with your hands.

“The medium that makes you write less is usually the one that makes you understand more.”

My compromise, after all six apps: cards for thinking, plain-text files for keeping. No proprietary format, no lock-in, readable in fifty years on whatever we’re using then. The app is allowed to be disposable. The notes are not.

Tool of the week

Slipbox

A note app that stores everything as plain Markdown files in a folder you choose — so if it disappears tomorrow, your notes don’t. I used it this week to draft the card-vs-app outline you just read; it exported to a clean text file I dropped straight into my keep folder. The point isn’t the features. It’s the exit.
