Robin Sloan tells a story about a guy on his street who restores furniture in his
garage. Not for an audience — he's just working, with the door rolled up, and
because it's open you get to watch the sanding and the staining and the slow
becoming of a chair. Sloan calls it **working with the garage door up**, and offers
it as a gentler cousin of "build in public."

The distinction is the whole point. *Building in public* can curdle into
performance — the announcement, the launch, the thread. *Working with the garage
door up* is quieter: you'd be doing the work anyway, you've just chosen not to hide
it. No promise that it's good. No promise that it'll ship. Just the ordinary process
left visible.

That's the spirit I want these notes to have. A [[Learning in public|public]] note
shouldn't feel like a published essay; it should feel like glancing into the garage
and catching me mid-sand. Which is also why the seedlings stay up even when they're
rough — pulling them down to look polished would be rolling the door back down.
