A **digital garden** is a website you tend rather than publish. A blog is a stream:
posts arrive in order, each one finished, then they sink down the page and you
never touch them again. A garden is a space: notes are arranged by topic and by
how they connect, and you go back and revise them as your thinking changes.

The metaphor does real work. It gives you permission to put something half-formed
in public, because a garden is *supposed* to have seedlings in it. It reframes
"unfinished" from a failure into a growth stage — which is why every note here wears
a status of *planted*, *growing*, or *evergreen* (see [[Tending notes]]).

A few things make a garden feel like a garden rather than a pile of files:

- **Density of links.** Notes point at each other constantly, so reading is
  wandering, not scrolling. The connective tissue is the value.
- **[[Backlinks]].** When note A links to note B, B quietly learns that A exists.
  That two-way awareness is what turns a folder into a network.
- **Topic over time.** Nothing is sorted by date by default. The newest note isn't
  the most important one.
- **Learning out loud.** Most gardens are an act of [[Learning in public]] — the
  author thinking where you can watch.

The lineage runs from Vannevar Bush's *Memex* through Ward Cunningham's wiki to the
current crop of personal sites. If you want the practice underneath the aesthetic,
start with [[Evergreen notes]].
